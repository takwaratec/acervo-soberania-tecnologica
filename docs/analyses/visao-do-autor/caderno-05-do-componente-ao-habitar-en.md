## Abstract

This notebook examines the transition from a technically conforming component to housing capable of supporting everyday life. It cross-reads Brazilian studies on social perception, resettlement, serial housing, expandability, resilience, post-occupancy evaluation, and housing-policy continuity with Federal Law No. 11,888/2008, which guarantees free public technical assistance to low-income families. The sources converge on one point: delivering a unit is not enough to produce adequate housing. Standardized designs are modified because families change, work, provide care, cultivate, store, socialize, and generate income at home; when these practices are absent from diagnosis, later adaptations can generate conflict, cost, unhealthy conditions, and construction risks. A seven-stage cycle is proposed: territorial listening, shared diagnosis, needs-and-capabilities programming, reasoned solution selection, assisted design and construction, preparation for use and maintenance, and post-occupancy evaluation feeding back into design. Technology is not the center of the process: bamboo, hybrid components, or any alternative should be adopted only when they answer verified needs and when fabrication, application, maintenance, and replacement are compatible with local capabilities and professional accountability. The authorial contribution is a diagnosis–decision–evidence–responsibility–feedback matrix connecting the right to housing, situated knowledge, technical safety, and continuous learning.

**Keywords:** social housing; technical assistance; participatory diagnosis; adaptability; post-occupancy evaluation; adequate housing; autonomy.

## 1. Introduction

The first four notebooks followed a material and institutional progression. Notebook 1 examined socioecological preservation; Notebook 2 moved from conditioned culms to protected components; Notebook 3 analyzed territorial manufacturing; and Notebook 4 organized compliance, standards, and technical responsibility.

This progression does not, by itself, achieve the ultimate purpose of building. A component may be durable and compliant without the resulting dwelling supporting the daily lives of its occupants. This notebook addresses the central question:

> **How to convert a technically admissible solution into a housing process that listens, protects, and expands the capacity to live, work, care, adapt, and decide?**

## 2. Seven-stage cycle: from diagnosis to feedback

1. **Territorial listening and agreement:** defining participation, consent, decision channels, and recording divergences.
2. **Shared diagnosis:** combining technical observations with resident experience regarding site safety, environmental comfort, family trajectories, income generation, and maintenance capabilities.
3. **Needs and capabilities programming:** translating findings into functional, environmental, and spatial requirements while valuing local skills.
4. **Comparison and selection of solutions:** evaluating alternatives against clear criteria (suitability, safety, environmental quality, adaptability, life-cycle cost, territorial capacity, environmental impact, governance).
5. **Assisted design and construction:** translating choices into controlled technical documents and executing under professional supervision (ATHIS - Federal Law 11,888/2008).
6. **Delivery prepared for safe transformation:** providing built-as-constructed documentation, accessible manuals, modification boundaries, and maintenance contacts.
7. **Post-occupancy evaluation (POE) and feedback:** monitoring performance across seasons and family changes, feeding findings back into future designs and public policy.
