## Effectiveness, durability, toxicity, and circularity of treatments

> **Technology Readiness Level (TRL):** This notebook consolidates a **proof of concept (TRL-3)** for the informed selection of bamboo preservative treatments, based on a documentary scope review of 17 sources. The compiled evidence demonstrates mastery of the state of the art, identifies critical gaps, and proposes an experimental agenda directed towards advancing to **TRL-4** (laboratory environment validation). The text explicitly details what literature already supports and, with equal clarity, what still requires primary testing. It does not constitute a prescription for use, certification, approval, or commercial treatment recommendation.

## Abstract

This notebook examines what a corpus of 17 documents supports regarding thermal, chemical, and pyroligneous treatments applied to bamboo. The review was guided by the *Acervo Soberania Tecnológica* and expanded through a directed search for four primary research articles. Studies were compared across four inseparable dimensions: effectiveness against degrading organisms, physical and mechanical effects, environmental impact, and operational feasibility. No universally superior treatment was found. Moderate steam treatment provides the most promising convergence between lower moisture uptake, mechanical integrity, and improved durability, although responses depend on species, geometry, temperature, duration, and equipment. Borates have direct evidence of antifungal efficacy and may preserve mechanical performance, but they are leachable and require proper management of solutions, effluents, and residues. Tannin-boron formulations maintained laboratory resistance against fungi even after substantial boron leaching, without resolving scale and life-cycle questions. Pyroligneous liquid studies indicate biological activity under some conditions, but direct evidence for bamboo remains insufficient or inconclusive, while the chemical variability of condensates prevents treating them generically as safe products. The synthesis proposes that ecological preservation should be assessed through the function delivered over time, combining service life, structural safety, human and environmental exposure, repairability, local process control, and waste destination. A staged research agenda is presented to turn technological promises into verifiable comparisons.

**Keywords:** bamboo; preservation; heat treatment; borates; pyroligneous liquid; durability; life-cycle assessment; TRL-3; social technology.

## 2. Introduction

Bamboo can grow rapidly, provide culms with a high strength-to-weight ratio, and substitute raw materials of greater environmental pressure in specific applications. These properties, however, do not eliminate its susceptibility to moisture, fungi, termites, and powder-post beetles (*Dinoderus minutus*). Preserving it is necessary when application requires a service life exceeding the natural durability of the material, and when frequent replacement transfers costs, risks, and labor to users.

The problem begins when "preservation" is reduced to a single question: does the treatment kill or repel the target organism? A solution may reduce biological attack while simultaneously weakening the culm, consuming energy incompatible with local production, exposing workers to hazardous substances, generating untreated effluents, or preventing repair and reuse. It may also show good results in small strips and fail in full round culms, or work against a fungus without controlling the dominant insect in another territory.

Therefore, this notebook treats preservation as a problem of alignment between material, environment, process, and society. The central question is:

> **What evidence supports the effectiveness and socioenvironmental safety of thermal, chemical, and pyroligneous treatments applied to bamboo, and what gaps prevent their responsible selection?**

The answer matters to students, designers, producers, communities, and researchers who need to distinguish three different statements:

- a process altered a property in the laboratory;
- a treatment extended durability under a defined exposure condition;
- a preservation system is environmentally and socially adequate over its service life.

The corpus contains evidence for the first two statements in bounded situations. No single study covers the third.

## 3. Declared socioecological perspective

### 3.1. Authorial formulation

In this notebook, **socioecological preservation** is the capacity to maintain a useful function of bamboo for the required time without invisibly transferring treatment costs to workers, water, soil, future users, or communities with lower remediation capacity.

This formulation forms part of the **Takwara Universe**, the conceptual framework presented in the collection index, and is not a conclusion attributed to sources. It stems from five commitments:

1. bamboo is part of a productive and territorial system, not an isolated solution;
2. plant origin of an input does not guarantee harmlessness;
3. autonomy means mastering choice, measurement, maintenance, repair, and disposal, not merely having raw material;
4. biological efficacy does not automatically compensate for structural loss or contamination;
5. open and verifiable knowledge is an infrastructure of scientific sovereignty.

The role of bamboo in this perspective is strategic because an underutilized resource can connect management, training, work, and construction. Its social value, however, depends on the capacity to process it safely and to recognize when evidence does not yet allow an application.

> **Etymological and epistemological dimension of the term Takwara.** In the Tupi-Guarani (Old Tupi) linguistic matrix, the word *takwara* (or *takoara*) arises from the combination of `takoa` (stem, stalk, culm, rod) and `wara` (holed, perforated, hollow, with an inner cavity), literally meaning **"hollow rod"** or **"culm with internal cavity"**. This fistulosity is the physical reason for the plant's high mechanical efficiency, but also constitutes its primary biological vulnerability (accumulation of starch and moisture in the internode, creating an ideal microclimate for beetles and wood-decay fungi). The retrieval of the designation *Takwara* in the authorial framework expresses this synthesis: socioecological preservation consists precisely of protecting the hollow cavity of the rod without compromising material integrity.
>
> This understanding dialogues with the cultural frameworks of other peoples:
> - **In the Americas (Chibcha/Quimbaya):** the term *Guadua* (originally *Guasgua*) joins `gua` (water/river) and `dua` (stem), meaning **"water stem"** — a reference to watershed conservation and liquid retained within the internode;
> - **In Vietnam (*Tre*):** bamboo is designated as *Người bạn thân thiết* (**"the devoted friend of the farmer"**), a defensive community element of the village (*Lũy Tre*) accompanying life from cradle to grave;
> - **In India (*Vamsha* in Sanskrit):** the term means **"lineage"** or **"family"**, due to the continuous sprouting of rhizomes, while the *Bansuri* flute symbolizes the soul emptied of ego;
> - **In China (*Zhú* - 竹):** it represents the **"humble gentleman"**, whose hollow cavity (*Xīn Xū*) symbolizes an open mind and resilient flexibility;
> - **In Japan (*Take* - 竹):** the country's oldest prose literature (*Taketori Monogatari* — *The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter*, 10th century) recounts the discovery of the celestial Princess Kaguya-hime inside a glowing hollow culm, symbolizing purity and sacred gift hidden in matter, integrated into the *Sho-Chiku-Bai* triad (Pine, Bamboo, Plum) of longevity and unyielding strength.
>
> In all these traditions, bamboo is not a mere disposable extractive input, but a companion of life and community whose preservation demands respect for its physical and social nature.

## 4. Method

### 4.1. Review design

A **repository-guided documentary scope review** was conducted, suitable for mapping types of evidence, comparing experimental conditions, and locating gaps. The work is not presented as an exhaustive systematic review or meta-analysis: materials, organisms, treatments, and outcomes are heterogeneous, and the search originated from a previously constituted thematic archive.

### 4.2. Corpus formation

The corpus was closed for this version on July 15, 2026. Included were:

- 13 anchor documents already curated in the Repository, selected for directly addressing preservation, post-treatment properties, biodeterioration, *Dinoderus minutus* management, or environmental impact of treatment;
- 4 complementary primary research articles located via directed search based on gaps in the initial set: mechanical performance of octaborate, tannin-boron with leaching and fungi, antifungal comparison between traditional salt and borax/boric acid, and steam in full round culms at pilot scale.

Of the 17 documents, 15 report experimental research or quantitative modeling; one technical circular describes production and risks of pyroligneous extract; one lecture material was used only for vocabulary and operational context. Didactic sources did not support efficacy values.

### 4.3. Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Documents with verifiable authorship, title, date, or provenance and accessible full text were included. DOIs were recorded when existing, but their absence did not exclude academic works identified in their originals.

Excluded from primary inference:

- commercial claims without associated methodology;
- service life values without traceable primary testing;
- equivalences between untested species, products, or scales;
- environmental inferences based solely on terms like "natural", "bio", or "conventional-biocide-free";
- transposition of wood results to bamboo without indicating indirect evidence.

### 4.4. Extraction and comparison

For each document, the following were extracted: species or substrate, geometry, initial condition, treatment, temperature, duration, concentration, application method, control, target organism, physical-mechanical outcome, biological result, environmental indicator, scale, and limitations.

Interpretive weight was classified for the core question of this notebook, rather than as a general grade of work quality:

## 5. Results

### 5.1. Overview: evidence is fragmented

The main finding of the cross-analysis is not the superiority of one product, but the fragmentation of knowledge. Thermal studies measure moisture, shrinkage, composition, and bending well, but rarely measure organisms and life-cycle impact. Borate studies demonstrate biological efficacy or mechanical preservation, but leaching and waste alter environmental assessment. Pyroligneous studies test products of variable composition with controls that do not always isolate chemical effect from heat. Life-cycle assessment identifies an environmental hotspot but does not compare alternatives or incorporate achieved service life.

This fragmentation prevents binary questions like "which treatment is best?". The technically valid question is: **best for which species, form, organism, function, exposure, infrastructure, and service horizon?**

### 5.2. Heat treatments: a window exists, not a universal temperature

#### Low and high severity steam

Chung and Wang (2017) treated strips of *Phyllostachys makinoi* and *P. pubescens* at 120 °C for 6 hours. Steam reduced density and equilibrium moisture content (EMC), and tended to reduce MOE and MOR. Presence of outer skin was associated with higher mechanical values; peeling increased water absorption, although steam reduced absorption in peeled samples. As all samples received 2% KOH prior to treatment, the experiment does not isolate a material without alkaline pre-treatment (Chung & Wang, 2017. DOI: 10.1007/s10086-017-1647-y).

Wang et al. (2020) expanded the range to 140, 160, and 180 °C for 10 to 30 minutes, under saturation pressures of 3.6, 6.2, and 10 atm. EMC fell with severity, but at 180 °C for 30 minutes MOR and MOE were approximately 47% and 20% below control. Authors indicated 160 °C/30 min and 180 °C/10 min as trade-offs among measured variables; they did not perform biological or field tests (Wang et al., 2020. DOI: 10.1186/s10086-020-01899-8).

Choi et al. (2026) add a decisive piece: full round moso culms (3.7 m long) were treated at 120–180 °C in a 1.8 × 1.8 × 8 m pilot facility. MOR peaked at 140 °C (38.9% above control) and fell at higher temperatures. From 160 °C onwards, marked improvement against fungi and termites occurred. After 16 weeks, mass loss by *Trametes versicolor* fell from 15.40% in control to 5.51% at 160 °C and 2.76% at 180 °C; for *Fomitopsis palustris*, it fell from 66.03% to 32.89% and 6.93%, respectively. In tests with *Reticulitermes speratus*, loss was 5.15% in control, 4.62% at 160 °C, and 1.92% at 180 °C. However, 180 °C also caused cracking, localized collapse, and severe degradation. The results position 140–160 °C as a compromise window, not a rule transferable to all species or equipment (Choi et al., 2026. DOI: 10.1016/j.indcrop.2026.123304).

The set supports four conclusions:

1. reducing hygroscopicity is not equivalent to preserving strength;
2. the condition that maximizes strength is not necessarily that which maximizes biological resistance;
3. temperature, duration, pressure, and geometry interact;
4. extreme conditions act as a warning: higher severity may improve biological durability while destroying structural value.

#### Dry heat and glued laminated bamboo (Glubam)

In Costa's thesis (2015), *Dendrocalamus asper* strips were treated at 150 or 180 °C for 2 or 4 hours. Mass loss ranged from 11.47% to 14.86%. In strips, 180 °C/4 h significantly reduced MOE, and 180 °C/2 h and 180 °C/4 h reduced MOR relative to control. Laminated bamboo treated at 150 °C/2 h presented highest MOR/MOE averages, but many failures occurred in the glue line and the source contains internal inconsistency between text and statistical comparison table. The study did not measure biodurability (Costa, 2015. B.Sc. thesis — University of Brasília).

Results converge with steam: moderate ranges can preserve or elevate a specific property, while high severity increases mass loss and damage. But it cannot be asserted that 150 °C/2 h is universally ideal, nor that observed gains stem solely from heat, as laminate manufacturing introduced bonding variability.

#### Liquid media: PDMS and bio-oil

Zhang et al. (2020) heated moso strips in polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) at 120, 150, 180, and 210 °C for 3 hours. EMC, swelling, and wettability decreased; material darkened; severe conditions produced mechanical losses. Without a control group subjected to the same thermal curve in steam, air, or different oil, the thermal effect cannot be fully separated from the chemical effect of silicone. Leaching, toxicity, biodegradation, or life cycle were not measured (Zhang et al., 2020. DOI: 10.1039/d0ra08713k).

Pimentel (n.d.) described an exploratory dual-oil process with a hot rapeseed oil bath followed by a cold linseed oil bath. Five of six compression samples of *D. giganteus* cracked, while moso samples and tension specimens did not. The report demonstrates that species, geometry, and subsequent testing matter, but does not contain a full durability or performance program validating the process (Pimentel, n.d. PUC-Rio).

These studies help design controls. A liquid medium cannot be called ecological based on plant origin or absence of conventional biocides; it must be characterized regarding consumption, reuse, emissions, retention, migration, equipment cleaning, and end of life.

### 5.3. Borates: consistent efficacy, conditional on water and waste

#### Mechanical performance

Gauss, Kadivar, and Savastano Jr. treated *D. asper* strips by vacuum/pressure with disodium octaborate tetrahydrate (DOT) at 5% and 8%. Full penetration and retentions of 14.79 and 21.79 kg/m³ were achieved. Dynamic MOE, static MOE, and compression modulus were statistically equivalent among groups; specific compressive strength increased 35.3% and 30.6%, and MOR variation was not statistically significant. The paper demonstrates that, under these conditions, treatment did not sacrifice measured mechanical performance. It did not test biodurability or leaching (Gauss, Kadivar, & Savastano Jr., 2019. DOI: 10.1186/s10086-019-1804-6).

#### Antifungal efficacy

Mwanja et al. compared crude lake salt (used by Ugandan artisans) with a 2%/6% borax/boric acid mixture on *Oxytenanthera abyssinica*, *Oldeania alpina*, and *Bambusa vulgaris*. After exposure to *Gloeophyllum trabeum*, *Coniophora puteana*, and *T. versicolor*, all borax/boric acid conditions were classified as very durable (Class I under European criteria). Crude salt ranked between Classes II and III, with mass losses from 5.9% to 14.24%. The study shows a traditional practice can be tested without idealization: cultural legitimacy does not replace performance against target organisms. Authors emphasize borates are water-soluble and recommend use protected from leaching (Mwanja et al., 2024. DOI: 10.1016/j.bamboo.2024.100092).

#### Tannin fixation

Gauss et al. (2021) investigated tannin, hexamine, and boron solutions on *D. asper*. In leaching cycles, boron loss was high: approximately 82.4% in the boron-only group and 81.7% in the selected tannin-boron formulation. Nevertheless, tannin conditions (leached or unleached) remained classified as durable or very durable against *Pycnoporus sanguineus* and *G. trabeum*, outperforming untreated bamboo and boron-only groups after leaching. Authors attribute remaining effect to polymerized tannin. Mechanical tests indicated positive effects (highest MOR/MOE in tannin-hexamine-boron group), and thermal analysis showed higher carbonaceous residue in treated conditions. The result is promising, but large boron loss shows "fixation" does not mean complete immobilization, and formulation, penetration, and reaction require further research (Gauss et al., 2021. DOI: 10.1016/j.conbuildmat.2021.122723).

#### Environmental impact of artisanal supply chain

Araujo et al. (2025) modeled cradle-to-gate artisanal production of 1 kg of treated *Guadua angustifolia* in Rio Branco, Brazil. The observed bath contained 100 L water, 5 kg borax, and 5 kg boric acid. Under normalization, human carcinogenic toxicity accounted for ~93% of potential impact, and contaminated waste (offcuts and discarded culms) accounted for 91% of total modeled impact. These numbers are not clinical cancer incidence; they are potential model outputs for a specific inventory and facility. The study does not compare alternative treatments, lacks full sensitivity analysis, and ends at the gate, without accounting for service life, use, or disposal (Araujo et al., 2025. DOI: 10.14295/bjs.v4i4.719).

Cross-analyzing the four articles corrects two opposing simplifications. It is incorrect to claim borates are ineffective merely because they leach: they demonstrated direct antifungal efficacy. It is also incorrect to call them an ecological solution solely due to lower mammalian toxicity relative to historical preservatives: solubility, waste, exposure, and solution disposal remain materially relevant.

### 5.4. Pyroligneous liquids: biological signal is not preservative validation

The term "pyroligneous liquid", "acid", or "extract" covers condensates whose composition depends on biomass, pyrolysis temperature, collection, standing, and tar separation. Treating all such products as equivalent compromises reproducibility.

Embrapa's Technical Circular 65 (Campos, 2007) describes raw condensate as a complex mixture and warns of tar and benzopyrenes. Its value in this review is procedural: collection temperature, decantation, and separation condition quality. The document is not an efficacy trial or safety certificate (Campos, 2007. ISSN 1981-5999).

In *D. giganteus* strips, Matsuoka and Beraldo (2014) applied pure product and dilutions (5–30%) via heated immersion for 15–180 minutes, followed by 6- and 16-week soil burial. Mass and ultrasound did not adequately distinguish soil, concentration, or duration; degradation was minor. Chemical characterization, heated water control, and reference preservative were lacking. The study did not demonstrate optimal concentration or conclusive fungicidal efficacy (Matsuoka & Beraldo, 2014. DOI: 10.5380/rf.v44i1.30220).

In Silva's thesis (2011), Glubam treated with diluent and pyroligneous solutions at 10%, 20%, and 30% was exposed to *D. minutus* for 20 weeks. No statistical difference supported a dilution over control. Moisture confounded mass variations, ultrasound poorly discriminated attack, and 10 of 11 attacked specimens showed damage at the node region. The result is valuable precisely for preventing an undue positive conclusion (Silva, 2011. M.Sc. thesis — UNICAMP).

Two indirect studies help separate biological activity from preservative performance. Martins (2017) found *in vitro* fungal inhibition by *Hovenia dulcis* liquor, but recommended distinguishing fungistasis from fungicidal action and testing impregnated material (Martins, 2017. B.Sc. thesis — UTFPR). In *Pinus elliottii* wood, Laroque (2022) observed fungal losses of 26.07% in control, 14.09% after autoclave, and 10.47% after immersion; for termites, 3.68%, 0.83%, and 1.31%. Attack was reduced, but raw liquid did not meet the preservative adequacy criterion used (Laroque, 2022. M.Sc. thesis — UFPel). Neither culture plates nor pine wood prove bamboo protection.

Therefore, the corpus supports pyroligneous liquid as a **research candidate**, not a validated bamboo preservative. The next scientific step must start with product identity: source biomass, thermal curve, collected fraction, pH, density, water, acids, phenols, and priority contaminants. Without this, "pyroligneous" is too broad a category for replication.

### 5.5. Management of *Dinoderus minutus*: treating the system, not just the culm

Silveira's thesis (2015) brings together distinct experiments on trapping, cashew nut shell liquid (CNSL), and electromagnetic exposure. In repellency tests, 0.05 and 0.09 mg/mL CNSL differed from control, with 0.09 mg/mL producing the highest response; 100% projection at 0.1 mg/mL is extrapolation, not observation. Mortality was not proven, and the work indicates the need for tests closer to field conditions, cost, durability, and environmental risk. The electromagnetic experiment showed strong differences in attack and population growth, but comparing one treated chamber with one control chamber can confound treatment effect with chamber effect (Silveira, 2015. Ph.D. thesis — UCDB).

The most robust contribution of the thesis to this notebook is methodological: rearing, trapping, and monitoring target organisms forms part of preservation infrastructure. Without this capability, absence of damage may result from low attack pressure rather than treatment. Nodal damage concentration observed by Silva (2011) also suggests that the analysis unit should preserve anatomy and geometry relevant to application.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1. "Natural" describes origin; safety requires measurement

Vegetable oil, tannin, pyroligneous liquid, traditional salt, and bamboo are materials linked to biomass or local practices. This origin may offer advantages, but does not answer dosage, composition, emission, corrosivity, persistence, ecotoxicity, or exposure. Embrapa's circular shows a plant condensate can carry tar and benzopyrenes; the CNSL thesis notes operational properties requiring caution; Amazonian LCA shows a bamboo chain can concentrate impact in treatment and waste.

The inverse is also true: a chemical treatment should not be rejected solely by its class. Borates demonstrated measurable efficacy and mechanical compatibility under defined conditions. Ecological judgment must compare function and risk using the same unit of analysis.

### 7.2. Thermal compromise is a multivariable problem

Wang et al. (2020), Costa (2015), and Choi et al. (2026) converge on a pattern: mechanical response has a peak or preservation range, while biological resistance tends to grow with severity until degradation of the bamboo itself becomes dominant. It is not a choice between "efficacy" and "failure", but locating an admissible operational region.

This region cannot be copied as a recipe. Pilot-treated full moso culms responded differently from *D. asper* strips, and peeling altered water and bending in Chung & Wang. Species, age, culm position, nodes, skin, initial moisture, heating rate, pressure, duration, and cooling must accompany any recommendation.

### 7.3. Functional unit must include time and performance

The 1 kg treated bamboo unit used by Araujo et al. (2025) allows identifying process impacts. To compare preservation treatments, however, output mass at the gate is insufficient. A higher initial impact process may reduce replacements; another, with low impact per kilogram, may fail early or lose structural capacity.

As an authorial methodological proposal, future LCAs should test a functional unit close to:

> **amount of bamboo capable of delivering the required mechanical function and exposure class for one year of verifiable service.**

This requires scenarios for maintenance, repair, replacement, reuse, and end of life. It does not mean assuming more durable treatment is always better; it means making explicit the service being compared.

### 7.4. Circularity is not automatic return to nature

Treated bamboo cannot be classified as ordinary organic waste without considering added substances. Offcuts and rejected culms may carry borates, silicone, oil, polymerized tannin, or pyroligneous fractions. Solution reuse reduces consumption only if concentration, contamination, and performance are monitored. Burning or pyrolyzing treated material requires dedicated evaluation of emissions and ash.

In this notebook, circularity means maintaining value with traceability: preventing scrap, designing for maintenance, separating components, recording treatment, prioritizing repair, and defining safe disposal before initiating the process.

### 7.5. Technical autonomy requires instruments and governance

A process strengthens autonomy only when its parameters can be understood and controlled by operators. This includes measuring moisture, temperature, duration, concentration, retention, or mass gain; logging batch and species; verifying equipment safety; observing attack pressure; inspecting cracks; and managing baths, effluents, and waste.

Pressure equipment can produce uniform penetration, but creates capital, maintenance, and safety requirements. Immersion may be more accessible, but demands time, tanks, solution control, and spill protection. Steam dispenses with added preservatives, but requires energy, insulation, thermal control, and crack prevention. Territorial decision-making must make these requirements visible, rather than shifting them to the community after technological choice.

## 8. What evidence supports — and what it does not yet support

### 8.1. Supported statements

- Heat treatments alter hygroscopicity, dimensions, chemistry, and strength of bamboo; response is non-monotonic.
- Steam around 140–160 °C presented the best compromise in pilot moso study, whereas 180 °C increased biological resistance alongside structural damage.
- Borax/boric acid was more effective against tested fungi than traditional salt across three bamboo species.
- Vacuum/pressure 5% and 8% DOT preserved measured mechanical moduli in *D. asper* strips.
- Borates are susceptible to leaching; tannin formulation did not eliminate boron loss.
- Waste and effluents from treatment can dominate potential impact of a modeled artisanal supply chain.
- Available direct evidence does not validate pyroligneous liquid as a general bamboo preservative.

## 9. Decision framework for research and design

Before selecting a treatment, the responsible party must answer:

1. **Function:** will bamboo be structural, cladding, furniture, landscaping, or replaceable component?
2. **Exposure:** protected, subject to occasional moisture, weather, ground contact, or water?
3. **Target organism:** decay fungi, mold, termites, *D. minutus*, or local combination?
4. **Material:** which species, age, position, node presence, skin, geometry, and moisture?
5. **Minimum performance:** which properties must remain after treatment and aging?
6. **Process:** temperature, pressure, time, concentration, and retention measurable and repeatable?
7. **Safety:** what protection, emissions, leachates, effluents, and waste arise?
8. **Maintenance:** can element be inspected, repaired, replaced, and tracked?
9. **Evidence:** does reference study reproduce material, use, organism, and scale, or is it analogy?

### Bounded situations supported by corpus

- **Water-protected use:** borates form most solid antifungal reference in corpus, provided leaching and bath management are treated as part of system. Does not replace specific beetle testing.
- **Full round culms in exterior use:** moderate steam is a promising line integrating mechanical gain and durability at pilot scale, but requires energy, climate, biological, and local species validation.
- **Post-leaching protection:** tannin-boron deserves comparative development because it maintained biological effect despite large boron loss; must not be presented as leaching-resistant formulation.
- **Pyroligneous liquid, dual-oil, CNSL, and emerging physical methods:** remain research lines. Must not be used as sole barrier in critical applications without corresponding testing.

## 10. Proposed experimental agenda — advancing from TRL-3 to TRL-4

The following agenda operationalizes transition from proof of concept (TRL-3) to laboratory environment validation (TRL-4). Each stage is designed to isolate variables, produce comparable metrics, and feed subsequent stages with objective passage criteria.

### 10.1. Stage 0 — defining use case

Select species, origin, age, culm position, geometry, structural or non-structural function, climate, exposure class, and target organism. Record acceptance criteria prior to testing.

### 10.2. Stage 1 — comparative screening with responsive controls

Minimum set must include:

- untreated control;
- process control, such as heated water or additive-free steam when applicable;
- reference borate with measured concentration and retention;
- moderate thermal range and severe condition used as damage limit;
- pyroligneous product only after batch chemical characterization;
- oil or polymeric medium with equivalent thermal group enabling separation of heat and additive.

Conditions must be randomized and replicated across multiple culms. Test specimen and experimental unit must not be confused: multiple samples from one culm do not replace culm diversity.

### 10.4. Stage 3 — aging and pilot scale

Treatments passing screening must be evaluated after leaching, moisture cycles, UV radiation when relevant, and prolonged biological exposure. Pilot scale must use final geometry — especially full round culms — and record thermal gradients, penetration, cracks, base-to-top variation, and node effects.

### 10.5. Stage 4 — decision by delivered service

Only after prior modules should life-cycle and cost comparison be performed with maintenance and replacement scenarios. Reports must publish null and adverse results. Reliable social technology grows when communities inherit decision protocols and auditable data, not promises.

## 11. Research questions for TRL-3 → TRL-4 transition

Questions below translate review gaps into testable hypotheses with minimum metrics and acceptance criteria. Each question corresponds to a Work Package (WP) in the experimental program:

**WP-1. Moderate steam on Brazilian species:**
*Hypothesis:* Moderate steam treatment of full round culms of *Bambusa vulgaris* or *Dendrocalamus asper* at 140–160 °C produces biological durability gain without exceeding 15% MOR/MOE loss relative to control.
*Metric:* MOR, MOE, fungal mass loss (*T. versicolor* & *G. trabeum* per ASTM D2017 or equivalent), EMC.
*Acceptance criterion:* Fungal mass loss < 10% and MOR ≥ 85% of control.

**WP-2. Borate leaching in full round culm geometry:**
*Hypothesis:* Boron retention in bamboo culms treated by 5% borax/boric acid immersion is sufficient to maintain Class I durability rating (EN 350) after 14 days of leaching (EN 84).
*Metric:* Retention before/after leaching, durability classification, fungal mass loss.
*Acceptance criterion:* Class I or II after leaching with boron loss < 60%.

**WP-3. Characterization and activity of bamboo pyroligneous liquid:**
*Hypothesis:* Pyroligneous liquid produced from *B. vulgaris* residues at 350–450 °C exhibits total organic acids ≥ 3% and total phenols ≥ 1%, inhibiting *in vitro* mycelial growth of *T. versicolor* with IC50 < 2% (v/v).
*Metric:* pH, density, total organic acids (titration), total phenols (Folin-Ciocalteu), IC50 in *in vitro* assay.
*Acceptance criterion:* IC50 < 2% without total inhibition of negative control.

**WP-4. Comparative efficacy of tannin-boron on neotropical species:**
*Hypothesis:* Tannin-hexamine-boron formulation on *D. asper* maintains Class I or II durability rating against *P. sanguineus* after leaching, with boron loss < 70%.
*Metric:* Fungal mass loss, boron retention, EN 350 rating, residual MOR/MOE.
*Acceptance criterion:* Class I or II rating with mass loss < 10%, MOR > 80% of control.

## 12. Limitations of this review

- Search was guided by Repository contents and complemented in a targeted manner; it does not cover all databases, languages, and preservation technologies.
- Studies use different species, ages, culm parts, geometries, organisms, and standards; combined effect size was not calculated.
- Part of corpus comprises non-peer-reviewed academic works and reports, included for verifiable identity and experimental contribution.
- Some works measure only one dimension of the question, preventing complete environmental or operational judgment.
- The 2026 pilot article is recent and offers a single facility and species; results need independent replication.
- Review does not substitute chemical legislation, occupational safety, structural standards, or required testing for specific applications.
- Quantitative hypotheses proposed in Section 11 (Research questions) are authorial formulations based on identified gaps; they do not constitute experimental results or validated performance estimates.

## 13. Conclusion

Preserving bamboo ecologically is not finding a "green" substance, but governing a trade-off between durability, integrity, risk, and local capability. Examined literature shifts debate from treatment names to verifiable conditions.

Moderate steam presents, within this corpus, the strongest experimental convergence between mechanical and biological performance, especially with advancing full round culm trials. It is not automatically simple, cheap, or low-impact: energy, equipment, and species must enter comparison. Borates provide a clear reference for antifungal efficacy and mechanical compatibility, but water reveals their limit, making effluents and waste a central part of technology. Tannin-boron showed promising residual protection, though failing to prevent ~82% boron leaching in analyzed study. Pyroligneous liquid possesses signals justifying research, not general validation; plant origin does not remove obligation to characterize composition and contaminants.

**Position in R&D program:** This scope review constitutes proof of concept (TRL-3) for the bamboo–bio-based PU structural system preservation axis. Proposed experimental agenda in Section 10 and quantified hypotheses in Section 11 provide roadmap for laboratory validation phase (TRL-4), funding for which is subject of present proposal. By explicating what literature supports and what requires primary testing, the notebook reduces technological risk of subsequent stage and establishes objective criteria for advancing, correcting, or halting each line of inquiry.
