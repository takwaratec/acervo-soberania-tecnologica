## Abstract

This notebook examines the transition from conditioned bamboo to a finished construction component. It connects prior evidence on fibre alkalinization, acidic correction, pyroligneous liquids, biodeterioration, castor-oil-derived polyurethanes, coatings, waterproofing, and rigid-foam-filled tubes. The authorial contribution is to organize these modules into an integrated research architecture: alkaline conditioning using an ash-derived solution; investigation of correction using characterized pyroligneous liquid; protected drying and storage; application of bio-based polyurethane; internal filling when functionally required; and component finishing. No source in the corpus assessed the complete sequence, and no original experiment validates it. NaOH studies show that alkaline treatments may modify interfaces and may also damage fibres when excessive; they do not provide transferable pH values or recipes for culms, ash solutions, and bio-based polyurethane. Pyroligneous liquids vary in composition and biological activity. Bamboo and castor-oil polyurethane have been combined experimentally, while absorption, voids, and adhesion remain critical. Foam filling may support tube walls in specific geometries and may also alter failure mode. This edition establishes public precedence for the conceptual architecture without converting plausibility into performance and without disclosing the experimental core reserved for research and development.

**Keywords:** bamboo; bio-based polyurethane; castor oil; alkalinization; pyroligneous liquid; rigid foam; interface; defensive publication; social patent.

## 1. Introduction

Bamboo treatment does not end when a preservative agent touches the culm. If the material is to be dried, stored, coated, filled, connected, and exposed in a building, each subsequent operation can preserve, undo, or transform the effects of the preceding one.

A biologically protected culm may retain moisture incompatible with resin curing. An alkalinized surface may favor a specific interface while suffering damage under more severe conditions. A coating may reduce water ingress while trapping residual moisture. A foam may support the wall and increase load capacity in one geometry, while rendering failure more brittle. A finish may extend exposure limits while impeding inspection, repair, or end-of-life separation.

Therefore, the unit of analysis in this notebook is not an isolated substance. It is the **component as a system of interfaces**.

The central question is:

> **What prior evidence makes the integration between bamboo conditioning, bio-based PU, internal filling, and finishing investigable, and which interfaces must be demonstrated before the component can be specified with confidence?**

## 2. Authorial concept made public

In the **Takwara Universe**, the research architecture organizes six functions:

1. **Alkaline conditioning:** treatment with ash-derived alkaline solution to modify surface energy and reduce starch/sugar content without destroying structural integrity;
2. **pH adjustment and biological stabilization:** investigation of neutralization/acidic adjustment using characterized pyroligneous liquid;
3. **Protected drying:** controlled moisture reduction to levels compatible with resin application;
4. **Polyurethane application:** coating or matrix impregnation with castor-oil-derived bio-based PU;
5. **Internal filling:** structural or thermal core filling with bio-based rigid foam when functionally required;
6. **Finishing and UV protection:** protective outer layer against weathering and solar radiation.

No source in the corpus evaluated all six stages sequentially in full round bamboo culms.

## 3. Interfaces and state of the art

### 3.1. Fibre and culm alkalinization
Alkaline treatment with NaOH is widely documented for natural fibres (Wong et al., 2010; Salih et al., 2020; Kamaruddin et al., 2022). Moderate concentrations (1–5%) can remove hemicellulose and lignin, increasing surface roughness and mechanical interlocking with polymer matrices. Excessive concentrations (>5–10%) degrade cellulose microfibrils and reduce tensile strength. Transferring fiber mesh parameters to whole bamboo culms or ash solutions requires measuring pH, exposure time, temperature, and penetration depth.

### 3.2. Pyroligneous liquid for pH correction
Pyroligneous extract contains organic acids (mainly acetic acid) and phenolic compounds (Campos, 2007; Matsuoka & Beraldo, 2014). While its acidic nature can neutralize residual surface alkalinity, its chemical variability demands strict batch characterization. Biological signals observed in vitro or in wood (Martins, 2017; Laroque, 2022) do not validate raw pyroligneous liquid as a standalone preservative for bamboo against *Dinoderus minutus* or decay fungi (Silva, 2011; Silveira, 2015).

### 3.3. Castor-oil bio-based polyurethane (PU)
Vegetable polyurethanes synthesized from castor oil offer high hydrophobicity, chemical resistance, and renewable origin. When combined with bamboo particles or fibers (Lopes, 2020; Zhang et al., 2018), bio-PU demonstrates strong mechanical adhesion and water resistance. However, interfacial bonding depends heavily on moisture content (<12%) and surface cleanliness.

### 3.4. Foam filling in tubular geometries
Filling hollow bamboo culms with rigid polyurethane foam (Wei et al., 2022) prevents localized wall buckling under axial compression, transforming buckling failure into progressive crushing. The functional benefit depends on wall thickness, culm diameter, foam density, and core-to-bamboo adhesion.

## 4. Synthesis of technical risks and passage criteria

| Interface / Operation | Main technical risk | Minimum passage criterion (TRL-3 → TRL-4) |
|---|---|---|
| **Alkaline conditioning** | Fibre degradation, loss of shear strength | Tensile/shear loss < 10% relative to untreated control |
| **Pyroligneous neutralization** | Uncontrolled pH, chemical contamination | Standardized pH (4.5–5.5), absence of tar/benzopyrenes |
| **Bio-PU coating** | Delamination due to high moisture | Moisture < 12%, pull-off adhesion > 2.0 MPa |
| **Rigid foam core** | Internal voids, moisture trapping | Core density uniformity, zero internal condensation |
| **Fire performance** | Increased flammability | Compliance with ABNT NBR 9442 and fire safety standards |
